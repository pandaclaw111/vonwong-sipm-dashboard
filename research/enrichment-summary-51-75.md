## Key Patterns in Cases #51-75

### 1. The Legal Pathway Is the Most Reliable European/Global North Strategy
Cases #57 (Urgenda), #58 (Germany), #55 (Iceland), and #54 (Poland) demonstrate that legal strategies in robust rule-of-law contexts consistently produce more durable outcomes than street protest alone. The Urgenda template has replicated in 30+ countries.

### 2. Treaty Cases Have the Highest Ceiling — and the Hardest Floor
Montreal Protocol (#61) is the model; Plastics Treaty (#64) is the cautionary tale. The difference: Montreal had (a) a visible visceral harm, (b) alternatives that were profitable for industry, (c) a small number of key states, (d) an extraordinary diplomatic leader (Mostafa Tolba). Plastics has none of these fully.

### 3. Economic Concessions Without Structural Reform Are the Default Failure Mode
Yellow Vests (#52), Greece (#59), Morocco Boycott (#51): governments routinely offer targeted economic concessions (fuel tax reversal, price cuts) that defuse movements without addressing structural causes. Consumer boycotts and street protest are most vulnerable to this.

### 4. Movement-to-Institution Conversion Takes 5-10 Years
Spain 15-M (#56): 2011 occupation → 2014 Podemos → 2015 Mayors of Change → 2020 government. Poland Women's Strike (#54): 2016-2020 protests → 2023 electoral victory → 2024-2025 policy change. The organizational infrastructure built during protest periods is the most durable product.

### 5. The ACT UP Model Remains Underdeployed
ACT UP (#68) + TAC South Africa (#69) represent the most replicable high-impact model in the dataset: combine (1) scientific/policy literacy within the affected community, (2) targeted corporate/institutional pressure, (3) specific winnable demands, (4) radical visual identity (Silence = Death). The AIDS movement's descendants are the most successful in this section.

## Benjamin Von Wong's Intervention Points in These Cases

### Highest Relevance — Direct Visual Gap Identified:

**Plastics Treaty (#64):**
The production-cap argument needs visual representation. "Plastic production tripling by 2050" is abstract. A Benjamin installation showing current production vs. 2050 projected production in physical terms could serve as the catalyst that makes the treaty fight visceral. *This is the single highest-priority visual opportunity in these 25 cases.*

**Pacific Islands — ICJ Timing (#73):**
ICJ advisory opinion expected mid-2025. A visual campaign documenting Pacific Island climate reality timed to the opinion's release would serve both advocacy and legal evidence functions. The timing window is narrow and specific.

**Fossil Fuel Divestment (#71):**
The "carbon bubble" mathematics need visual translation. What does $40T in committed divestment look like vs. $1T in continued fossil fuel investment? The scale disparity between what has been divested and what is still being invested would make compelling large-scale visual work.

**Bangladesh Climate Litigation (#72):**
Visual documentation of Bangladesh's climate-displaced communities would serve as both advocacy and legal evidence. The intersection of garment supply chains (Benjamin's existing work) and climate displacement is unique to Bangladesh.

### High Relevance — Supporting Visual Infrastructure:

**ACT UP Model (#68 + #69):** The "Silence = Death" design tradition directly informs Benjamin's practice — art embedded in life-or-death advocacy, made by people deeply embedded in the affected community.

**Anti-Apartheid Divestment (#74):** The fossil fuel divestment movement directly descends from apartheid divestment. The visual language that worked for apartheid (isolation, stain on corporate reputation) is being reapplied to fossil fuels.

**Urgenda + Germany Legal Cases (#57 + #58):** Benjamin's visual work could serve as legal evidence in climate litigation. "Slow violence made visible" is exactly the type of documentation that climate courts need.
