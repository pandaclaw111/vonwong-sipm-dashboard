## WHAT THIS MEANS FOR BENJAMIN VON WONG

### The Big Picture

Benjamin sits at a **rare intersection**: he's a technically world-class photographer with documented ability to create viral, emotionally resonant images that drive real policy conversations — AND he has an explicitly activist methodology, not just "issue-adjacent" aesthetics. This is genuinely uncommon. Most photographers are either technically excellent without movement connection, or movement-connected without the production value. He has both.

The question isn't "how does Benjamin access movements?" — the question is **"which movements are most underserved by visual storytelling, and where is philanthropic money flowing toward communications/cultural strategy?"**

### Where Benjamin Should Focus: The Under-Visualized Movements

**1. Ocean / Marine Conservation — HIGHEST PRIORITY**
- The dominant imagery in ocean conservation is underwater photography (Cristina Mittermeier, Paul Nicklen)
- What's missing: **above-water, human-scale visualization of ocean threats** — the fishing industry's human face, deep-sea mining, the supply chain of what ends up in oceans
- Benjamin's plastic work (Drowning in Plastic, etc.) already proved the formula
- **Who to call**: Oceana, Blue Marine Foundation, Packard Foundation ocean team, Pew Charitable Trusts oceans program

**2. Fashion / Supply Chain Justice — ESTABLISHED TERRITORY BUT UNDERFUNDED**
- Benjamin IS the best-known visual activist in this space
- The Laudes Foundation is the primary dedicated funder and explicitly funds communications/campaign work
- **Gap**: The human faces of garment workers are almost never visualized at scale with the production quality that breaks through
- **Who to call**: Laudes Foundation directly; Clean Clothes Campaign; Fashion Revolution; Remake

**3. Food Systems — ALMOST NO VISUAL ACTIVISM AT SCALE**
- The food systems movement is intellectually rich (Vandana Shiva, Michael Pollan) but almost entirely text-driven
- The industrial food system — factory farms, seed monocultures, supply chains — has almost no visual language except undercover video
- **Opportunity**: Create the visual language for food systems justice the way Benjamin created the visual language for plastic pollution
- **Who to call**: McKnight Foundation food team; Rockefeller Foundation True Cost of Food initiative; IATP

**4. Digital Rights & Surveillance — INVISIBLE PROBLEM NEEDS VISUALIZATION**
- The digital rights movement suffers from a fundamental visualization problem: surveillance, data harvesting, and digital authoritarianism are *invisible*
- Whoever creates the breakthrough visual metaphor for digital surveillance will define this movement's cultural moment
- **Who to call**: Access Now, EFF, Omidyar Network/Luminate, Ford Foundation technology team

**5. Indigenous Rights — UNDERFUNDED ON VISUAL STORYTELLING**
- Indigenous rights orgs are chronically underfunded compared to their importance
- High-production-value visual campaigns by non-indigenous ally artists are done sensitively and with community co-authorship — but almost no one is doing it at scale
- Norwegian government (NICFI) and Rainforest Foundation Norway are spending heavily in this space and need communications work
- **Who to call**: Amazon Watch, Forest Peoples Programme, Rainforest Foundation Norway

### Strategic Recommendations

**1. Position Activism.Studio as the visual infrastructure layer for movements**
Rather than individual commissioned projects, pitch Activism.Studio as the visual communications partner that gives movements the imagery they can't produce themselves. Movements have message; you have medium. This makes the business model more durable than project-by-project.

**2. The Laudes Foundation is the most direct money**
They fund the intersection of fashion + sustainability + campaign work. They know what good work looks like. They're not going to need a 20-minute explanation of why fashion industry visuals matter. Start here.

**3. The OSF Soros Justice Fellowship is worth applying for**
It's explicitly for artists doing justice work. It's individual funding (not org funding). It would provide a year's salary equivalent plus platform and network. Apply in the next cycle.

**4. Build relationships with the connective tissue organizations**
Ford Foundation, MacArthur, and OSF all have program officers who span multiple movements. One good relationship with a Ford Foundation program officer who funds both environmental AND racial justice work opens multiple doors simultaneously.

**5. The under-visualized movements need a breakthrough image, not a campaign**
The most powerful thing Benjamin did wasn't run a campaign — it was create an image that *became* a campaign. The food systems movement, the digital rights movement, and the indigenous rights movement are all waiting for their "Drowning in Plastic" moment. Whoever creates that image will define those movements' visual identity for a decade.

**6. The ocean-plastic-fashion nexus is where multiple funders overlap**
Ocean funders (Packard, Bloomberg, Pew), fashion funders (Laudes), and climate funders (ClimateWorks, Bezos Earth Fund) all care about ocean plastic as it relates to their domains. A project at this intersection could be funded from multiple philanthropic streams simultaneously.
