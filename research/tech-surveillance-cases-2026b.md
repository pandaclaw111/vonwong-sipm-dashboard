### #T2-01 Google Antitrust Remedy Phase (United States, 2024–2026) [K]

**Issue:** Whether the world's largest search monopoly should be broken up — and what that reveals about AI market concentration.
**SIPM Score:** Grievance 4 | Narrative 3 | Infrastructure 4 | Permission 3 | Trigger 4 = **18/25**

**Build-up:** US vs. Google search monopoly ruling arrived August 2024 — Judge Amit Mehta found Google to be an illegal monopolist under Section 2 of the Sherman Act. DOJ proposed remedies including potential divestiture of Chrome and Android, mandatory data sharing with competitors, prohibition on exclusive default search deals (worth $26B/year from Apple alone). Google's defense: structural remedies would destroy innovation; behavioral remedies are sufficient. AI Now Institute warned in September 2025 that the ruling "failed to accurately examine the broader AI market" — the case barely touched Google's dominance in AI infrastructure, cloud, and the emergent AI search transition.

**Legislative hook:** DOJ remedy trial underway through 2026. Final remedy ruling expected mid-to-late 2026. Parallel: EU DMA enforcement against Google's self-preferencing in search (ongoing). EU fined Google €2.4B for shopping search (2017), €4.34B for Android (2018); DMA adds new enforcement powers.

**Movement pressure:** Open Markets Institute (Barry Lynn) arguing for structural breakup. Electronic Frontier Foundation focused on privacy implications of behavioral vs. structural remedies. AI accountability advocates pushing for remedy to include AI market restrictions — Google controls DeepMind, YouTube AI training data, and Search AI Overviews. The argument: a behavioral remedy without addressing AI market consolidation reproduces the monopoly one layer up.

**Key Orgs:** Open Markets Institute (openmarketsinstitute.org), AI Now Institute (ainowinstitute.org), EFF, Center for Democracy & Technology

**Upcoming trigger:** Remedy ruling (mid-2026); if DOJ drops or weakens remedy under Trump administration (politically likely) — backlash trigger from civil society.

**Benjamin's Opportunity:** The visual gap — **a single Google logo at the center of a web connecting every product, service, and AI system it now controls**: Maps, YouTube, DeepMind, Chrome, Android, Gmail, Search, Gemini, Waymo, Fitbit, Waze. Make it look like a nervous system — or a surveillance octopus. The antitrust case is invisible to most people because Google is invisible: it's the water they swim in. The image needs to make the monopoly *felt*, not just described.

**Connection to existing SIPM cases:** Connects to Big Tech antitrust, AI market concentration, democracy (control of information infrastructure), AI surveillance (all Google products collect behavioral data feeding AI training).

### #T2-02 Meta Teen Mental Health — The Social Media Harm Litigation Wave (United States, 2024–2026) [K]

**Issue:** Whether Meta (Facebook, Instagram) knowingly designed addictive features that harmed children's mental health — and whether a corporation can be held liable for algorithmic harm.
**SIPM Score:** Grievance 5 | Narrative 5 | Infrastructure 4 | Permission 4 | Trigger 4 = **22/25**

**Build-up:** The Jonathan Haidt/Jean Twenge academic case (2015-2023) documented teen mental health decline coinciding with smartphone/social media adoption. Frances Haugen's whistleblower testimony (2021) revealed internal Meta research showing Instagram knew it harmed teenage girls. The Social Dilemma (2020) created mainstream awareness. State AGs began coordinating. By 2023: 40+ state attorneys general sued Meta. Federal multi-district litigation (MDL) consolidated in Northern District of California. School districts across the US filed separate suits against Meta, TikTok, Snap, YouTube, Google — now 100+ school districts.

**2026 status:** Trial dates beginning to be set. First California state trial expected 2026. The MDL has survived Meta's motion to dismiss. Key legal theory: Meta knew about harm and concealed it (product liability + fraud framing, not Section 230 platform liability). New Mexico AG released discovery documents showing Meta's internal knowledge of exploitation — including findings that Instagram's recommendation algorithms connected adult men to accounts of underage girls within minutes of following minors. This is potentially the **Big Tobacco moment** for social media.

**Legislative hook:** Kids Online Safety Act (KOSA) passed Senate 2024 but died in House. State-level: California's Age-Appropriate Design Code (AADC) challenged in court. Social Media Safety laws in multiple states face First Amendment challenges. Arkansas, Utah, Texas social media minor laws all in litigation. This legislative-judicial-AG pincer is coordinating.

**Key Orgs:** Center for Humane Technology (Tristan Harris), ParentsTogether, American Psychological Association (APA), state AGs coalition (40+), Institute for Justice (First Amendment angle)

**Movement infrastructure:** Social media safety has become genuinely bipartisan — rare alignment of religious conservatives (protecting children from sexual content) and progressive parents (mental health). Jonathan Haidt's "The Anxious Generation" (2024) became a movement text.

**Upcoming trigger:** First California state trial verdict (2026); any new internal Meta document release; Congressional hearing on child exploitation discovery findings.

**Benjamin's Opportunity:** The visual gap — **a visualization of the Gap between what Meta showed Congress and what it knew internally**. Two rooms: one the Senate hearing room where Zuckerberg sat, one a server farm where teen behavioral data flows through recommendation algorithms. The distance between the public face and the private machine. Or: an installation of thousands of teen faces — representing the millions of underage users — displayed as data points in an algorithm's recommendation graph, with red lines connecting them to adult accounts. The horror is in the graphic.

**Connection to existing SIPM cases:** Directly connects to democracy (platform power over public discourse), AI surveillance (recommendation algorithms as behavioral manipulation systems), deepfakes (NCII targeting minors).

EU Digital Markets Act (DMA) came into force March 2024 designating Apple, Meta, Google, Amazon, Microsoft, ByteDance as "gatekeepers." Apple opened iOS to alternative app stores (EU only) — but with new "Core Technology Fee" (€0.50/install above 1M) that critics called a DMA compliance workaround. European Commission opened formal proceedings against Apple in 2024.

**2026 status:** EU fined Apple €500M in March 2025 for non-compliance with DMA browser/search requirements. Additional App Store investigation ongoing. Developer movement: Coalition for App Fairness (Spotify, Epic, Match Group) active in Brussels. **Legislative hook:** EU DMA enforcement proceedings against Apple (European Commission). Epic v. Apple US appeal — Supreme Court could take the case. UK CMA App Store investigation parallel.

**Key Orgs:** Coalition for App Fairness (coalitionforappfairness.org), EFF, Open Markets Institute, European Digital Rights (EDRi)

**Upcoming trigger:** EU Commission final decision on Apple DMA App Store compliance (2026); any developer publishing a high-profile alternative iOS app store.

Amazon Labor Union (ALU) won Staten Island (JFK8) in April 2022 — the first US Amazon union. Subsequent organizing at more facilities. Congressional investigations ongoing. EU Platform Work Directive (see Case #T2-09) specifically addresses algorithmic management of gig workers — and is beginning to spill into warehouse context. Amazon challenged by unions in 7+ countries simultaneously.

**Legislative hook:** NLRB rulings on Amazon's anti-union conduct (multiple cases). EU Platform Work Directive (2024, implementation by 2026). State warehouse worker algorithm disclosure laws (California, NY).

**Key Orgs:** Amazon Labor Union (amazonlaborunion.org), UNI Global Union, GMB (UK), International Brotherhood of Teamsters, Make Amazon Pay coalition

**Upcoming trigger:** Any ALU contract negotiation becoming public; Amazon warehouse death attributable to TOT pressure (has happened multiple times, each is a potential trigger); UK legislation on algorithmic management.

### #T2-07 Data Scraping / Robots.txt Battles — The Consent Frontier (Global, 2023–2026) [K]

**Issue:** Whether companies scraping the public web for AI training must respect opt-outs — and whether "publicly available" data requires consent to use for commercial AI training.
**SIPM Score:** Grievance 4 | Narrative 3 | Infrastructure 3 | Permission 2 | Trigger 3 = **15/25**

**Build-up:** The robots.txt standard (1994) was designed for search engine crawlers — a voluntary convention, not law. AI training scrapers ignore it or create dedicated crawlers that circumvent it. Web publishers have discovered that AI training bots consume 50-100x more bandwidth than search crawlers. Major publishers: New York Times sued OpenAI and Microsoft for training data use (December 2023) — case ongoing. The Associated Press, Reuters, and Getty Images have signed licensing deals with AI companies instead. The scraping-vs-licensing divide is splitting the media industry.

**2026 status:** OpenAI and Cloudflare have both released new AI bot identification standards. The "opt-out registry" concept (analogous to Do Not Call) is being proposed but has no legal force yet. European Data Protection Board clarified GDPR applies to web scraping of personal data for AI training. California Privacy Protection Agency (CPPA) proposed regulations on automated decision-making that could affect training data. New York Times lawsuit still in discovery. Media organizations lobbying for "publisher's right" analogous to EU news aggregator law.

**Legislative hook:** NYT v. OpenAI/Microsoft (SDNY — trial expected 2026); EU AI Act transparency requirements; GDPR enforcement on personal data in training sets; proposed US federal AI training data legislation.

**Key Orgs:** News Media Alliance, Digital Content Next, Electronic Frontier Foundation (pro-scraping for research), Center for Democracy & Technology (nuanced position)

**Upcoming trigger:** NYT lawsuit trial or settlement; EU regulatory action on major AI company for GDPR training data violation; Congressional hearing on AI training data compensation.

Cities banning: Santa Cruz (2020), Los Angeles (2020, partial), New York City (2022, partial), New Orleans (2021). LAPD formally abandoned PredPol 2020. But other cities are expanding. EU AI Act classified "real-time biometric identification in public spaces" as prohibited — but predictive policing tools classified as "high risk" (allowed with oversight, not banned).

**2026 status:** Mixed: some US cities have banned, others secretly continued (EFF documented cities asking neighboring jurisdictions to run the same queries). Clearview AI settled its Illinois BIPA class action ($51.75M, March 2025). New York City Local Law 49 (2023) requires city agencies to disclose automated decision tools — being challenged. Under Trump, federal pushback on local bans expected. ACLU challenging predictive policing as Fourth Amendment violation. ICE expanding predictive enforcement algorithms for immigration.

**Legislative hook:** ACLU lawsuits in multiple jurisdictions; NYC Local Law 49 implementation; EU AI Act high-risk AI obligations (2025-2026); proposed federal Stopping Mass Harms from Automated Systems and Targeting (SMART) Act.

**Key Orgs:** ACLU (aclu.org/ai), Algorithmic Justice League (ajl.org), Brennan Center for Justice, Stop LAPD Spying Coalition, Mijente

**Upcoming trigger:** Any documented wrongful arrest directly traceable to predictive algorithm output; Chicago SSL legal challenge; federal rollback of local bans under Trump.

### #T2-09 Gig Worker AI Management — The Platform Work Directive (Europe/Global, 2023–2026) [K]

**Issue:** Uber, Lyft, DoorDash, and Deliveroo manage their workforces entirely via algorithm — surge pricing, rating systems, route optimization, deactivation — with no human in the loop. The EU's Platform Work Directive is the first law to directly regulate algorithmic management.
**SIPM Score:** Grievance 5 | Narrative 4 | Infrastructure 4 | Permission 3 | Trigger 4 = **20/25**

**Build-up:** 28 million gig workers in the EU are algorithmically managed. A Deliveroo rider can be deactivated mid-shift by an algorithm. An Uber driver can be deactivated for a 4.7-star rating. No appeals process. No human review. The UK Supreme Court ruled in 2021 that Uber drivers are "workers" entitled to minimum wage and holiday pay — Uber's UK algorithm had been found to have fabricated records. Spain's "Riders Law" (2021) gave gig workers employee status. EU Platform Work Directive passed February 2024, requiring: legal presumption of employment for platform workers; prohibition on certain algorithmic decisions without human review; right to challenge automated decisions.

**2026 status:** EU Platform Work Directive implementation deadline: member states must transpose by mid-2026. Early implementation legislation in France, Germany, Italy showing varied interpretations. US: no federal equivalent; California Prop 22 (2020) — Uber/Lyft ballot measure classified gig workers as contractors — survived legal challenges; Lyft-Uber-DoorDash spent $224M to pass it. Illinois, Massachusetts, New York considering similar legislation. WA state passed first-in-nation app-based driver protections 2022.

**Legislative hook:** EU Platform Work Directive transposition (2026); California AB-5 ongoing legal challenges; proposed federal WORKER Act; UK Supreme Court Uber ruling implementation.

**Key Orgs:** International Alliance of App-Based Transport Workers (IAATW), SEIU, UNI Global Union, Independent Workers of Great Britain (IWGB), Gig Workers Collective

### #T2-11 Biometric Data Privacy — The BIPA Litigation Wave (United States, 2008–2026) [K]

**Issue:** Illinois' Biometric Information Privacy Act (BIPA) — the strictest biometric law in the US — creating a massive wave of class action litigation and a test of whether privacy violations without "actual harm" can be legally remedied.
**SIPM Score:** Grievance 5 | Narrative 3 | Infrastructure 5 | Permission 4 | Trigger 3 = **20/25**

**Build-up:** BIPA passed in Illinois in 2008 (first US state biometric law) after financial data breaches. It requires: written consent before collecting biometric identifiers (fingerprints, facial geometry, voiceprints, retina scans); written retention policy; no selling biometric data; damages of $1,000-5,000 per violation. Tech companies and employers routinely ignored it. In 2019, Illinois Supreme Court ruled plaintiffs don't need to prove actual harm — just a violation. This opened the floodgates. BIPA litigation: Facebook settled $650M (2021), TikTok settled $92M, Clearview AI settled $51.75M (2025), Google settled $100M for Google Photos facial tagging in Illinois.

**2026 status:** BIPA litigation is still active but Illinois legislature has been under intense corporate lobbying pressure to weaken it. In 2023, IL Supreme Court ruled that each individual scan is a separate violation (White Castle case — multiplying potential damages to billions of dollars). McDonald's, White Castle, and retail companies pushing for legislative cap on damages. Other states: Texas and Washington have biometric privacy laws (weaker than BIPA). Washington My Health MY Data Act (2023) extends beyond biometrics to all health data. Federal biometric legislation proposed but not passed.

**Legislative hook:** Illinois legislature BIPA amendment battles (ongoing, corporate vs. civil society); Washington My Health MY Data Act implementation (2024-2026); proposed federal BIPA-equivalent legislation.

**Key Orgs:** ACLU of Illinois, EFF, Clearview AI plaintiffs' counsel (Edelson PC), EPIC, National Consumers League

**Upcoming trigger:** IL Supreme Court or legislature weakening BIPA (corporate lobbying pressure); White Castle damages trial; any major new BIPA defendant (Amazon, Apple, or federal agency).

**Benjamin's Opportunity:** **A visual inventory of every biometric scan one person undergoes in a day**: face unlock on phone (dawn), building entry scan (morning), Disney World ride photo (vacation), employer fingerprint check-in (work), TSA face scan (airport), hospital iris scan, stadium face recognition, store loyalty program facial recognition, driver's license facial recognition, social media photo tag — all rendered as a day-in-the-life visual timeline. By evening, 20+ biometric captures. Each one labeled by which company now holds permanent rights to that biometric data. The accumulation IS the story. Benjamin's documentary style would make this visceral.

**Connections:** AI surveillance, predictive policing, immigration enforcement (biometrics at border), gig worker management (biometric check-ins), digital colonialism (biometric data export rules).

### #T2-12 SpaceX / Starlink Sovereignty & Surveillance Concerns (Global, 2020–2026) [K]

**Issue:** A private company controls the world's largest satellite constellation, providing critical communications infrastructure for governments, militaries, and underserved populations — with one person (Elon Musk) able to turn it on or off.
**SIPM Score:** Grievance 3 | Narrative 4 | Infrastructure 2 | Permission 3 | Trigger 3 = **15/25**

**Build-up:** Starlink has 6,000+ satellites (January 2026, expanding rapidly). It provides internet to Ukraine's military — Elon Musk acknowledged restricting Starlink near Crimea in 2023 to prevent a drone strike, effectively intervening in military operations based on his personal decision. Starlink has been used by indigenous communities in remote areas, by disaster relief, and by authoritarian regimes to surveil traffic patterns (who is communicating with whom). The US military is a major Starlink customer. Romania, France, and other NATO allies have expressed concern about dependence on a private satellite network controlled by a single individual. SpaceX has US government contracts worth $15B+.

**2026 status:** Growing congressional concern after Musk's simultaneous position as DOGE head, major Trump donor, and Starlink operator. Senators have questioned whether Musk's government role creates conflicts with Starlink contracts. EU is developing its own satellite constellation (IRIS²) partly to reduce Starlink dependence. No regulatory framework exists for private satellite network operators and their ability to selectively deny service. SpaceX has data on all Starlink users — a surveillance dataset that would dwarf any traditional surveillance system. Starlink terminals have been found in Russian forces — US export control violations.

**Legislative hook:** Senate DOGE oversight hearings; export control violations investigation; proposed legislation to require military satellite diversification; EU IRIS² development timeline.

## What's the Compound Story?

Across these 19 cases, a single thesis emerges with increasing clarity:

> **The infrastructure of convenience is the infrastructure of control.**

Every tool that makes modern life easier — the targeted ad, the ride-hailing app, the online exam, the social media feed, the AI search query — is simultaneously a tool of surveillance, management, extraction, and control. The convenience is real. The control is invisible. They run on identical infrastructure.

The compound story is not about any one company, any one technology, or any one policy battle. It's about the **design pattern** that underlies the entire digital economy:

**Collect everything → Make it convenient → Sell the convenience → Sell the data → Rent the surveillance to whoever will pay, including governments.**

This design pattern now touches:
- How we work (Amazon warehouse, Uber, gig platforms)
- How we learn (EdTech surveillance)
- How we vote (deepfakes, algorithmic polarization)
- How we breathe (AI data centers consuming water and energy in drought zones)
- How we're policed (predictive algorithms, facial recognition)
- How we're deported (CBP buying our ad-tech location data)
- How we create (AI trained on scraped artwork without consent)
- How we communicate (internet shutdowns, encryption backdoors)

The movements fighting this are fragmented because the **harms look different** — an artist losing income, a warehouse worker losing bathroom breaks, a teenager losing mental health, an immigrant losing their home, a community losing its water. But the **machine causing all of them is the same machine**.

The person doesn't look up. They see none of this. The phone glows pleasantly.

**Title:** *The Convenience Layer*
