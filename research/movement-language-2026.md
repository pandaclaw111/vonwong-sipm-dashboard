# Movement Language Research: Slogans, Hashtags, Metaphors & Symbols (2014–2026)
*Deep research for Benjamin Von Wong's SIPM project*
*Compiled: March 2026 | Sources: Wikipedia, academic analyses, news archives*

---

## Overview

This document maps the language, imagery, and symbols that crystallized social movements over the last decade — focusing on what spread, why it spread, and what failed. Each entry includes the primary hashtag, key slogan, central metaphor, viral visual, and analysis of the psychological mechanisms behind virality.

---

## #BlackLivesMatter (2013–ongoing)

**Primary Hashtag:** #BlackLivesMatter — coined July 13, 2013; peaked May–June 2020 with George Floyd protests; became one of the most-tweeted hashtags in history (~47.8 million tweets in a two-week period, summer 2020)

**Key Slogan:** "I Can't Breathe" — last words of Eric Garner, July 17, 2014; repopularized as last words of George Floyd, May 25, 2020

**Central Metaphor/Symbol:** The raised fist (Black Power salute) + the phrase as an assertion of humanity against systemic denial; "say their names" as a memorial ritual making abstract statistics into individual people

**Viral Visual:** A white NYPD officer in a chokehold on Eric Garner (2014), captured on video; later the image of Darnella Frazier's 9-minute 29-second video of Derek Chauvin kneeling on George Floyd's neck. In 2020: aerial photos of "BLACK LIVES MATTER" painted in giant yellow letters on 16th Street NW, Washington D.C., two blocks from the White House.

### Why It Worked
The hashtag was created by Alicia Garza, Patrisse Cullors, and Opal Tometi on July 13, 2013 in response to Trayvon Martin's acquittal — Garza wrote a "love letter to Black people" and Cullors added the hashtag. The phrase challenged a foundational premise (that Black lives were *not* being treated as mattering), using radical simplicity to force listeners to either agree or reveal their opposition. The grammatical construction is an argument and a declaration simultaneously — it implies the status quo denies this, which makes it inherently political without being jargon-heavy.

### What Failed
- **"All Lives Matter"** — emerged as a counter-slogan, but its proponents missed (or deliberately sidestepped) the rhetorical logic, making it function as a dismissal
- **"Defund the Police"** (see BLM 2020 entry) — alienated moderate supporters despite meaningful policy content; easily misrepresented as a call for lawlessness
- **"No Justice, No Peace"** — effective chant but less precise as a policy frame
- Early movement messaging lacked specific policy asks, which made it easy to support symbolically without institutional change

## #MeToo (2006/2017)

**Primary Hashtag:** #MeToo — used as hashtag from October 15, 2017 when Alyssa Milano tweeted it; spread to 85+ countries within days

**Key Slogan:** "Me Too" — two words, first person singular, present tense. Coined by Tarana Burke in 2006 on MySpace for survivors of sexual violence, especially young women of color.

**Central Metaphor/Symbol:** The phrase as a mirror — if you say "me too," you're reflecting back someone else's experience and saying "I see you; I've been there." The collective chorus created a flood metaphor: what seemed isolated revealed itself as everywhere.

**Viral Visual:** Alyssa Milano's October 15, 2017 tweet asking women to reply "Me Too" if they'd experienced harassment. Within 24 hours: 12 million Facebook posts. The sight of celebrity after celebrity (Gwyneth Paltrow, Ashley Judd, Jennifer Lawrence, Uma Thurman) saying two identical words created a visual wave of solidarity. The *Time* "Silence Breakers" cover (December 2017, Person of the Year) showing Harvey Weinstein accusers — the power of faces attached to voices.

### Why It Worked
The genius of "Me Too" is that it requires almost no courage to say — two words, no accusation named, no story required, just acknowledgment of shared experience. This lowered the barrier to participation exponentially. The phrase is also inherently empathetic: it shifts focus from the perpetrator to the survivor and says *you are not alone*. Burke's original intent was about empathy among survivors; Milano's viral use expanded this to demonstration of *scale*. The combination of celebrity credibility (Milano's platform) with intimate universality (two ordinary words) created unprecedented reach. Facebook reported ~45% of American users had a friend who posted #MeToo within 24 hours.

### What Failed
- Some counter-hashtags (#HimToo, #NotAllMen) attempted to shift focus to false accusation narratives
- The movement faced criticism for centering white, wealthy, Hollywood women while Tarana Burke's original focus on Black women and girls of color was initially sidelined
- Due process debates led to backlash among some who felt the movement moved too fast from accusation to consequence
- The 2-word purity of the slogan made it hard to develop into sustained policy change infrastructure

## Standing Rock / #NoDAPL (2016–2017)

**Primary Hashtag:** #NoDAPL — began April 2016 with Standing Rock Sioux youth organizing

**Key Slogan:** "Mni Wiconi" / "Water is Life" — Lakota language slogan connecting spiritual, physical, and political stakes simultaneously

**Central Metaphor/Symbol:** "Water Protectors" (not protesters) — a deliberate reframe that transformed the narrative from opposition to stewardship, placing indigenous people as guardians rather than obstructors. The prayer circle and the sacred fire became the visual center of the camps.

**Viral Visual:** (1) Attack dogs biting water protectors in September 2016 — a direct echo of 1960s Civil Rights imagery of police dogs attacking peaceful protesters, immediately readable across generations. (2) Military veterans kneeling before elders in December 2016, asking forgiveness for the US government's treatment of indigenous people — one of the most emotionally devastating images of the decade. (3) Water cannons used on protesters in freezing weather, November 2016.

### Why It Worked
The "Water Protectors" reframe was strategically brilliant — it positioned indigenous people not as anti-progress activists but as fulfilling a sacred duty to protect what sustains all life. "Water is Life" / "Mni Wiconi" used an indigenous language phrase that felt universal yet distinctly rooted. The use of Lakota language itself was a political act of indigenous sovereignty. The movement fused religious framing (prayer, ceremony, sacred ground) with environmental stakes and indigenous rights, creating a coalition of Native nations, environmentalists, and eventually 4,000 veterans. The physical presence of the Sacred Stone Camp created a media pilgrimage site — thousands traveled there, and thousands livestreamed.

### What Failed
- The movement won a temporary administrative victory (Army Corps halted permits) but ultimately lost when the Trump administration reversed the decision in January 2017
- The physical camp was evicted in February 2017; the pipeline was completed
- The movement's spiritual center also made it harder to mobilize those who didn't share that frame
- "Outside agitator" narratives were used to delegitimize non-Native supporters

## Marriage Equality / #LoveWins (2015)

**Primary Hashtag:** #LoveWins — exploded June 26, 2015 when the Supreme Court ruled in *Obergefell v. Hodges*; one of the most-used hashtags in Twitter's history at the time

**Key Slogan:** "Love Wins" — two words capturing an entire movement's victory; previously used by Rob Bell's 2011 theology book but repurposed here

**Central Metaphor/Symbol:** The rainbow — adopted from the rainbow flag created by Gilbert Baker in 1978. The rainbow simultaneously evokes joy, diversity, promise, and the universal (it appears after storms). It's the only social movement symbol built on a spectrum rather than a single color.

**Viral Visual:** The White House illuminated in rainbow colors on the evening of June 26, 2015 — Obama's White House lit up to celebrate the ruling. This image spread globally within hours and remains one of the most iconic photographs of the decade. Additionally, millions of people temporarily changed their Facebook profile photos to rainbow filter overlays (a Facebook-facilitated feature).

### Why It Worked
"Love Wins" is maximally elegant: it names what the movement is for (love), it names what movements require (winning), and it implies there was a fight in which something dark lost. The rainbow visual has cross-cultural resonance — it's not specific to one group's iconography. The Supreme Court ruling on June 26 (also the anniversary of *Lawrence v. Texas*, 2003, and *United States v. Windsor*, 2013) gave the movement a crystalline victory moment to celebrate rather than protest, which activated an entirely different emotional register: joy rather than anger. Facebook's profile filter mechanism enabled mass participation with one click, creating visual solidarity at unprecedented scale.

### What Failed
- The movement's messaging around "marriage equality" vs. "gay marriage" — "equality" framing was deliberately chosen to be more universal and less identity-specific, which worked
- "Religious freedom" counter-framing emerged as a powerful opposition tool
- The joy of #LoveWins obscured ongoing discrimination in employment, housing, healthcare that remained legal in many states

### Sources
- [Obergefell v. Hodges — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obergefell_v._Hodges)
- [Same-sex marriage in the United States — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Same-sex_marriage_in_the_United_States)

## Occupy Wall Street / "We Are the 99%" (2011–2012)

**Primary Hashtag:** #OccupyWallStreet, #Occupy — began September 17, 2011; spawned #OccupyEverywhere, #OccupyBoston, etc.

**Key Slogan:** "We are the 99%" — coined via economist Joseph Stiglitz's May 2011 Vanity Fair article "Of the 1%, by the 1%, for the 1%"; popularized through a Tumblr blog launched August 2011 by activist "Chris" and Priscilla Grim; David Graeber (anthropologist and anarchist) widely credited with helping develop the framing

**Central Metaphor/Symbol:** The 1% / 99% split — a mathematical expression of moral outrage. The number itself became the protest sign, the chant, and the analysis. Zuccotti Park as physical occupation: the idea that democratic space (a public plaza) could be reclaimed by citizens.

**Viral Visual:** The "pepper spray cop" — UC Davis police officer John Pike casually pepper-spraying seated, peaceful student protesters on November 18, 2011. The image spread globally and was Photoshopped into hundreds of historical paintings and photos (the Declaration of Independence, Monet's water lilies, the moon landing). This meme-ification extended the image's reach for years.

### Why It Worked
The 1%/99% framing was genius because it used quantitative language (statistics, math) to make an emotional argument. It drew a line — you're either with the 99% or you're with the 1% — that made it hard to be neutral. The number also implied structural analysis rather than blame toward individuals. The physical occupation gave the movement a place, an image, and a daily media focus. Adbusters (Canadian anti-consumerist magazine) initiated the call; the horizontal organizing structure meant no single point of failure.

### What Failed
- The movement is widely cited as the canonical example of **powerful slogan, weak infrastructure**: brilliant language, no specific demands, no organizational mechanism to convert energy into policy change
- The 99%/1% frame was too broad: the 99% includes people with wildly divergent interests (a middle-class homeowner and an unhoused person are both in the 99%)
- "General Assemblies" were lauded for radical democracy but paralyzed decision-making
- The movement was ultimately evicted from parks nationally in November 2011 with minimal lasting structural change
- However: **the conceptual vocabulary it created** (the 1%, income inequality, wealth gap) became mainstream political language that shaped Bernie Sanders 2016, Elizabeth Warren, AOC, and the broader progressive wing

- [We are the 99% — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/We_are_the_99%25)

## #FridaysForFuture / Greta Thunberg (2018–ongoing)

**Primary Hashtag:** #FridaysForFuture, #SchoolStrikeForClimate, #ClimateStrike — global peak September 20, 2019 with ~4 million strikers in 161 countries; second-largest climate mobilization in history

**Key Slogan:** "How dare you" — from Greta Thunberg's UN speech, September 23, 2019, New York. "You have stolen my dreams and my childhood with your empty words." Also: "Our house is on fire."

**Central Metaphor/Symbol:** The school strike sign — Greta's handmade cardboard sign reading "Skolstrejk för klimatet" (School Strike for Climate) outside the Swedish parliament, August 20, 2018. The handmade-ness matters: a teenage girl, alone, a piece of cardboard. Not a manufactured campaign, but a moral act. The phrase "Our house is on fire" (used at Davos 2019) made the climate crisis immediately legible through the most universally understood emergency metaphor.

**Viral Visual:** Greta Thunberg alone outside Sweden's Riksdag parliament, August 2018, holding her handmade strike sign. Then: the image of her staring at world leaders at the 2019 UN Climate Summit with "How dare you" and the visible anger of a teenager addressing adults who have failed to act.

### Why It Worked
A sixteen-year-old girl sitting alone outside a parliament building with a handmade sign is an inherently compelling image: it forces the question "why is this child doing what adults should be doing?" Her identity as a teenager with Asperger's syndrome also became part of the message — her diagnosis meant she could not be diplomatic or polite about the truth, and she positioned that as a moral advantage rather than a limitation. The school strike frame had tremendous psychological leverage: children sacrificing their education to protest adult inaction is a perfect moral inversion. "How dare you" at the UN was the most watched and memed moment of the 2019 climate moment — raw anger, on the world's most formal diplomatic stage.

### What Failed
- Greta-centrism created fragility: when Greta's popularity declined or she was attacked, the movement felt the impact
- The movement mobilized millions but produced no binding policy outcomes at the 2019 or 2021 climate conferences
- Climate anxiety became associated with the movement, leading to debates about whether despair was mobilizing or paralyzing
- "How dare you" energy created backlash from those who felt lectured or shamed, contributing to climate-skeptic countermobility

### Sources
- [Fridays for Future — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fridays_for_Future)
- [Greta Thunberg at the 2019 UN Climate Summit speech](https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2019/sep/23/greta-thunberg-un-climate-summit-speech-how-dare-you)

## Extinction Rebellion / XR (2018–ongoing)

**Primary Hashtag:** #ExtinctionRebellion, #XR, #TellTheTruth, #ActNow, #ClimateEmergency

**Key Slogan:** "Tell the Truth" (Demand 1), "Act Now" (Demand 2) — structured as three explicit demands: Tell the Truth, Act Now, Beyond Politics (citizen's assembly)

**Central Metaphor/Symbol:** The hourglass (extinction symbol) — a stylized hourglass inside a circle, designed to signal "time is running out for many species." Simple, reproducible anywhere, cross-cultural. Also: die-ins (mass theatrical lying-down-dead in public spaces) as a literal metaphor for mass extinction.

**Viral Visual:** Pink boats occupied in the middle of Oxford Circus, London, April 2019 — protesters locked themselves to a bright pink yacht in the middle of one of London's busiest intersections for 4 days. The incongruity of a pink sailboat on a city street created an image that functioned simultaneously as art, protest, and spectacle. Also: naked protesters in the UK Parliament in 2019 — a deliberate attempt to strip away pretense.

### Why It Worked
XR was deliberately designed using academic research on nonviolent civil disobedience (especially Erica Chenoweth's finding that movements needed 3.5% of the population to succeed). The hourglass symbol is graphically clean and communicates urgency across languages. The theatrical tactics (die-ins, pink boats, gluing to things, nakedness) guaranteed media coverage. The structure of three specific demands was more actionable than most movements. The "Declaration of Rebellion" — formally declaring against government — gave XR a theatrical gravitas.

### What Failed
- XR became deeply polarizing, especially in the UK: a 2019 YouGov poll found 54% opposed their tactics of disrupting roads and public transport
- Blocking commuters, protesters gluing themselves to subway trains, blocking ambulances — these actions alienated the moderate public they needed to convert
- The movement struggled with accusations of being overwhelmingly white and middle-class
- Roger Hallam (co-founder) made antisemitic statements in 2019, causing international XR chapters to distance themselves
- The 3.5% theory of change required XR to accept arrest en masse — which works until the state decides to simply not arrest protesters (thus removing the media spectacle)

- [Extinction Rebellion — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_Rebellion)

## #MarchForOurLives / #NeverAgain (2018)

**Primary Hashtag:** #NeverAgain, #MarchForOurLives, #EnoughIsEnough — March 24, 2018; 1.2–2 million participants in the US, largest single-day protest since the Women's March 2017

**Key Slogan:** "Never Again" — borrowed from Holocaust memory, reimagined for gun violence. Coined by Cameron Kasky (Parkland student) and his classmates four days after the February 14, 2018 Stoneman Douglas shooting.

**Central Metaphor/Symbol:** The school desk — protest signs shaped like school desks, empty chairs, rows of shoes representing each death. The counter-weapon: students themselves. The phrase "Thoughts and prayers" became the counter-slogan — a scathing name for political inaction that survivors wielded as a weapon against politicians.

**Viral Visual:** X González (survivor) speaking at the March for Our Lives in Washington D.C., crying silently on stage for 6 minutes and 20 seconds — the exact duration of the Parkland shooting. The silence communicated more than any speech. Also: "We Call B.S." — González's speech at a Fort Lauderdale rally, February 17, 2018, went viral as she called out politicians who accept NRA money.

### Why It Worked
The messenger was the message. When shooting survivors speak, it is categorically harder to dismiss than when advocates speak — any attempt to silence them backfires. The students were telegenic, articulate, media-literate (they understood social media viscerally), and their grief was visually immediate. "Never Again" borrowed from the most morally authoritative historical precedent for genocide prevention (Holocaust memory) — a powerful rhetorical move that elevated the moral stakes. "Thoughts and prayers" as a counter-slogan was particularly sharp: it named the *failure* of opponents, not just their difference, using their own language against them.

### What Failed
- "Never Again" had been used before by other gun-control groups; the historical resonance with the Holocaust also created some discomfort
- The movement produced the most activity at the state level but failed to dislodge the NRA's federal legislative power
- Some critics noted that Parkland's suburban, largely white, middle-class students received more media attention than Black and Brown victims of gun violence in urban areas — highlighting the inequity of whose grief is amplified

### Sources
- [March for Our Lives — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/March_for_Our_Lives)
- [Never Again MSD — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Never_Again_MSD)

## George Floyd / BLM 2020 Surge (2020)

**Primary Hashtag:** #BlackLivesMatter, #SayTheirNames, #GeorgeFloyd, #ICantBreathe — summer 2020 became the largest protest movement in US history by some estimates (15–26 million participants over several weeks)

**Key Slogan:** "Say their names" — naming Breonna Taylor, George Floyd, Ahmaud Arbery, Tony McDade, and hundreds of others. The ritual of naming as political and memorial act. Also: "I can't breathe" (Floyd's last words, also Eric Garner's 2014 last words).

**Central Metaphor/Symbol:** The knee on the neck — Derek Chauvin kneeling on George Floyd's neck for 9 minutes 29 seconds became both the literal cause and the central image. Pro-protest: protesters kneeling in solidarity (echoing Colin Kaepernick's 2016 NFL kneeling). Counter-image: police kneeling with protesters in early days (later seen as performative).

**Viral Visual:** Darnella Frazier's bystander video of George Floyd's murder — 9:29 of video that couldn't be unseen. Then: aerial and satellite photos of massive protest crowds in every major US city simultaneously. Then: "BLACK LIVES MATTER" painted in 35-foot letters on the street leading to the White House. And: the image of a lone protester, Ieshia Evans, in a dress, standing calmly before armored riot police in Baton Rouge, 2016 — this photo from Alton Sterling protests was recirculated and became iconographic again.

### Why It Worked
The video was irrefutable — nearly 10 minutes, unedited, of a police officer with his knee on a Black man's neck while bystanders begged him to stop. In a media environment saturated by viral controversy, this was viscerally inarguable to most viewers. The simultaneity of protests across all 50 states and in 60 countries demonstrated the universality of the outrage. "Say their names" had profound humanizing power — it forced acknowledgment of specific individuals rather than abstract statistics. The movement also had 7 years of organizational infrastructure from 2013–2020 that could be activated instantly.

### What Failed
**"Defund the Police"** — this is the canonical example of the last decade of a policy-accurate slogan that was politically catastrophic.
- Popularized by Black Visions Collective shortly after Floyd's death; adopted by BLM chapters
- To its proponents, "defund" meant reallocate (not abolish), redirecting funds to social services, mental health, housing
- To the general public and all political opponents, it sounded like eliminating police
- A May 2021 poll showed only 18% support and 58% opposition
- Democrats distanced themselves from the phrase rapidly; Republicans used it as a campaign weapon through 2022
- The lesson: **a slogan with a complex explanation attached to it is a vulnerable slogan**. If your first sentence after the slogan is "what we really mean is...", the slogan has failed.

## Essential Workers / COVID-19 (2020–2021)

**Primary Hashtag:** #EssentialWorkers, #EssentialButExpendable, #ClapForCarers (UK)

**Key Slogan:** "Essential but Expendable" — the slogan that captured the fundamental contradiction: workers deemed essential enough to risk death, paid too little to be valued

**Central Metaphor/Symbol:** The contradiction itself is the metaphor: "essential" (we need you to keep going) vs. the treatment they received (low wages, no PPE, no sick leave). The clapping ritual in the UK (Clap for Our Carers, every Thursday at 8pm) as an initially moving, then deeply critiqued gesture — symbolic appreciation as a substitute for material change.

**Viral Visual:** UK residents on balconies clapping simultaneously during lockdown. Then: the counter-image — NHS nurses and healthcare workers protesting in scrubs for adequate PPE during the pandemic, some holding signs reading "We're clapping for pay, not clapping for claps." Healthcare workers sleeping in their cars to protect families. The Amazon warehouse worker counting packages, the grocery store clerk bagging food without hazard pay.

### Why It Worked
"Essential but Expendable" is a pure rhetorical chiasmus — the tension between the two words is the argument. Any essential worker can immediately understand the critique. The clapping ritual created a powerful initial moment of solidarity that later became a source of critique, demonstrating how a symbol can be coopted or revealed as hollow when not backed by material change. The contradiction between "we call you heroes" and "we won't give you PPE/raises/sick leave" became the defining tension.

### What Failed
- The "hero" framing ("healthcare heroes," "frontline heroes") was widely criticized for romanticizing exploitation — the same dynamic as using military language to avoid addressing conditions
- Clap for Carers became a symbol of performance over policy in the UK
- The movement produced significant cultural awareness but limited lasting wage or labor protection gains for most essential workers
- No single hashtag achieved the staying power of other movement language — the crisis was too diffuse

### Sources
- ["Essential but Expendable" — labor movement analyses, 2020](https://www.jacobinmag.com/2020/04/essential-workers-coronavirus-pandemic-labor)
- [Clap for Our Carers — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clap_for_Our_Carers)

## #StopCopCity / Defend the Atlanta Forest (2021–2025)

**Primary Hashtag:** #StopCopCity, #DefendTheForest, #DefendAtlantaForest

**Key Slogan:** "Forest Defender" — for those physically occupying the Weelaunee Forest (South River Forest) to prevent construction of the Atlanta police training facility

**Central Metaphor/Symbol:** The forest itself — ancient urban forest as sacred, communal, climate-critical space; urban nature as the front line where police militarization and environmental destruction converge. Tortuguita (Manuel Esteban Paez Terán), killed by police in January 2023, became a martyr — "Turtles don't run" became a phrase honoring their memory.

**Viral Visual:** Aerial footage of bulldozers entering the forest. Tortuguita's face — a young, smiling activist in a tree — memorialized on murals. "Stop Cop City" spray-painted along the Proctor Creek Greenway Trail. The coalition image: environmentalists and police-accountability activists together in the woods.

### Why It Worked
"Stop Cop City" was unusually effective because it used the city's own nickname for the training facility ("Cop City") — language coined by opponents using opponents' informal language as an accusation. The movement fused two usually separate audiences: climate/environmental defenders and police accountability activists, creating an unusual coalition. The physical occupation of a forest translated environmental stakes into concrete, photographable resistance. Tortuguita's killing in January 2023 drew international attention and outrage that sustained the movement.

### What Failed
- The domestic terrorism charges against protesters (61 indicted) successfully chilled organizing and fundraising
- The facility was ultimately completed and opened in April 2025 despite years of opposition
- The petition for public referendum was tied up in legal challenges that were never resolved before construction completed
- "Outside agitator" framing was used against protesters who came from outside Georgia

- [Stop Cop City — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stop_Cop_City)
- [Killing of Tortuguita — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Killing_of_Manuel_Esteban_Paez_Ter%C3%A1n)

## Iran / Mahsa Amini — "Woman, Life, Freedom" (2022–2023)

**Primary Hashtag:** #MahsaAmini, #WomanLifeFreedom, #JinJiyanAzadi, #OpIran

**Key Slogan:** "Jin, Jîyan, Azadî" (Kurdish) / "Woman, Life, Freedom" (English) — a Kurdish political slogan that became the rallying cry of the largest Iranian protest movement since the 1979 Islamic Revolution

**Central Metaphor/Symbol:** Hair — women cutting their hair publicly became the defining visual act of the movement. Hair under the hijab represented the state's control over women's bodies; cutting it was a public act of reclamation. The hijab itself, burning in protest fires, became the central symbol. Both acts are deeply personal made political.

**Viral Visual:** Women cutting their hair in front of crowds, on camera, in streets across Iran and in diaspora protests worldwide. The image of Iranian women removing hijabs and burning them in the street. A lone woman standing on a utility box, hijab removed, waving it above her head in the middle of a Tehran street — this iconic image was taken in 2022 during the Amini protests.

### Why It Worked
"Woman, Life, Freedom" is a slogan of remarkable compression and universality. Each word is independently powerful; together they form a complete philosophical stance. Originating from the Kurdish freedom movement (Abdullah Öcalan's writings and Kurdish women's movement since the late 20th century), it already had decades of meaning, but became immediately legible to anyone. The hair-cutting and hijab-burning acts created visuals that required no translation and no caption — they communicated the stakes of the protest (freedom from state control of women's bodies) through a single physical gesture. The slogan spread internationally because "Woman, Life, Freedom" is universally resonant — these three words need no context.

### What Failed
- The protests were ultimately suppressed; at least 551 protesters were killed, ~19,000+ arrested
- The political leadership of Iran remained unchanged by spring 2023
- Internet shutdowns and arrests of movement leaders prevented coordination
- The movement lacked a unified political program beyond the protests themselves

### Sources
- [Woman, Life, Freedom — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Woman,_Life,_Freedom)
- [Mahsa Amini protests — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahsa_Amini_protests)

## Gaza / Palestine Solidarity (2023–ongoing)

**Primary Hashtag:** #FreePalestine, #CeasefireNow, #GazaGenocide, #AllEyesOnRafah — "All Eyes on Rafah" (posted on Rafah refugee camp bombing in May 2024) became one of the most-shared Instagram posts in history, reaching 47 million shares within 24 hours

**Key Slogan:** "Ceasefire Now" — urgent, present tense, one clear demand. Also: "From the River to the Sea, Palestine will be Free" — deeply contested; viewed as a call for liberation by supporters, as a call for elimination of Israel by opponents.

**Central Metaphor/Symbol:** The watermelon 🍉 — used as a coded symbol of Palestinian solidarity since at least 2007 (artist Khaled Hourani for "Subjective Atlas of Palestine"), with origins tracing to 1980 when an Israeli art gallery displaying Palestinian flag colors was shut down. The watermelon's red, green, white, and black interior matches the colors of the Palestinian flag. Used to evade content moderation while signaling solidarity. Also: the keffiyeh (traditional Palestinian scarf) — worn globally as a solidarity symbol. The olive tree — a recurring Palestinian symbol of rootedness and displacement.

**Viral Visual:** "All Eyes on Rafah" — an AI-generated image of a vast refugee tent camp from above, with the text "All Eyes on Rafah." Despite (or because of) being AI-generated, it was shared 47 million times on Instagram within 24 hours after Israeli strikes on a Rafah displacement camp. Also: viral videos of Palestinians writing their names and those of family members on the legs and arms of children — labeling children in case they were killed and needed identification, a devastating image with no precedent.

### Why It Worked
"Ceasefire Now" worked because it is an immediate, actionable demand that anyone can understand — no political analysis required. The watermelon as coded symbol demonstrated ingenuity under censorship — social platforms removed Palestinian flag emojis and Arabic text, but the fruit emoji remained. This censorship itself became a story. "All Eyes on Rafah" worked partly because the simplicity of the AI image made it instantly replicable as a protest graphic. The visual scale of a vast tent city communicated displacement at a level that individual photographs couldn't.

### What Failed
- **"From the River to the Sea"** became the most contested slogan of the movement — supporters insisted it was about liberation and equality; opponents (including many Jewish communities) heard it as a call for elimination. The ambiguity, while perhaps intentional as a broad coalition phrase, created a persistent political crisis for the movement in Western contexts
- **"Genocide"** as a framing: calling the Gaza conflict genocide was hotly contested (legally, politically); while widely used by protesters and eventually by some international bodies, it polarized potential allies and was weaponized against protesters in legal and institutional settings
- Meta/Instagram censorship of Palestinian content was documented extensively, creating both a real suppression problem and a valuable narrative about platform power

### Sources

## Anti-Authoritarianism 2025 — "No Kings" / "Hands Off"

**Primary Hashtag:** #HandsOff, #NoKings, #WeWillNotComply, #DOGE (satirically), #TeslaBoycott

**Key Slogan:** "No Kings" — crystallized around the June 2025 protests, the largest US protest wave since 2020; derived from the core American founding anti-monarchical tradition. Also: "Hands Off" — as in: hands off Medicare, Social Security, democratic institutions, immigrants, reproductive rights.

**Central Metaphor/Symbol:** The crown as tyranny symbol — the "No Kings" frame invoked the American Revolution explicitly, attempting to make resistance to Trump feel like patriotism rather than partisanship. The "Hands Off" image is literally tactile — stop touching, stop dismantling, step back.

**Viral Visual:** Aerial photos of the April 5, 2025 Hands Off protests — up to 5 million participants across 1,400+ locations in all 50 states, the largest single-day protest in US history until the No Kings protests in June 2025. The Tesla boycott signs and "Tesladon't" imagery targeting Elon Musk. Town hall meeting confrontations with Republican legislators — ordinary constituents demanding answers in their local congressional offices, going viral on a near-daily basis.

### Why It Worked
"No Kings" works because it's maximally simple and invokes the most fundamental American political value — republican self-governance, opposition to monarchy. It makes resistance feel like constitutional patriotism. "Hands Off" works because it's imperative, direct, and adaptable — you can finish the sentence with anything ("hands off my Medicare," "hands off our elections," "hands off our bodies"). The protest movement of 2025 benefited from the infrastructure built by every previous movement — email lists, legal networks, organizational muscle from 2017 Women's March, BLM, Sunrise Movement, Indivisible, etc.

### What Failed
- The movement's diffuse nature (opposing many things simultaneously) made it hard to identify a single victory that would sustain momentum
- "We Will Not Comply" was effective as a civil disobedience frame but required sustained coordinated action that was difficult to maintain
- The Tesla boycott had mixed economic results; Musk retained his position
- The sheer volume of targets (DOGE, tariffs, ICE, reproductive rights, education cuts, etc.) risk diffusing energy

## Fast Fashion / Atacama Desert (2021–ongoing)

**Primary Hashtag:** #FastFashion, #SlowFashion, #WhoMadeMyClothes (Fashion Revolution Day), #SecondHandSeptember, #BuyLess

**Key Slogan:** "Who Made My Clothes?" — Fashion Revolution's tag, created after the 2013 Rana Plaza garment factory collapse in Bangladesh killed 1,134 workers. Workers inside factories photographed themselves holding signs answering back: "I Made Your Clothes."

**Central Metaphor/Symbol:** The Atacama Desert's textile graveyard — tens of thousands of tons of discarded clothing dumped in Chile's Atacama Desert, visible from satellites, photographed by drone in 2021. The image of a mountain of colorful, recognizable Western clothing items in one of the world's most remote and pristine environments became one of the most powerful environmental photographs of the decade. The visual paradox: the world's most fashionable waste in the world's driest desert.

**Viral Visual:** Drone and aerial footage of the massive clothing mountains in the Atacama, broadcast by AFP and AP wire services in 2021, republished globally. The clothes are identifiable — brand names, patterns, colors from stores anyone recognizes — sitting in a desert where nothing decomposes. Benjamin Von Wong's own fashion/waste installations are part of this visual tradition.

### Why It Worked
The Atacama images work because they provide specific, visible evidence of abstract waste statistics. "Fast fashion produces 92 million tons of textile waste per year" is a number. A mountain of jeans visible from space is an image. "Who Made My Clothes?" worked because it transforms a product into a relationship — it puts a human on the other side of every purchase. The Rana Plaza collapse provided the moral shock that made the question urgent. The answer campaign ("I Made Your Clothes" from workers in factories) created a loop of visibility and accountability.

### What Failed
- The fast fashion movement has not significantly slowed the industry: as of 2026, 36% of worldwide clothing purchases are fast fashion, and the total market is $178B/year
- "Sustainable fashion" has been thoroughly greenwashed — brands claim sustainability while increasing production
- Consumer-focused slogans (buy less, buy better, thrift) place responsibility on individuals rather than corporations or regulators, limiting systemic impact
- The Atacama images caused global coverage but no direct policy response

### Arab Spring Slogans (2010-2012)

**Egypt: "Bread, Freedom, Social Justice" (عيش، حرية، عدالة اجتماعية)**
- Arabic: Aish, Hurriya, 'Adala Igtima'iyya
- Origin: January 2011 Tahrir Square, became the most enduring slogan of the Egyptian revolution
- The trinity synthesizes economic demands (bread/aish), political demands (freedom/hurriya), and structural demands (social justice/adala)
- *Aish* means both "bread" and "life" in Egyptian Arabic — linguistic compression
- Sources: [Wikipedia: Arab Spring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_Spring)

**Tunisia: "Dégage!" (Get out! / ارحل)**
- Origin: December 2010, directed at President Ben Ali
- Power in single-word simplicity — a dismissal that requires no elaboration
- Template for other Arab Spring countries; "irhal" (ارحل) versions spread to Egypt, Syria, Yemen
- Demonstrates how imperative commands travel faster than explanatory demands
- Sources: [Wikipedia: Arab Spring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_Spring)

**Syria: "The people want the fall of the regime" (الشعب يريد إسقاط النظام)**
- Arabic: Al-sha'b yurid isqat al-nizam
- Adapted from original Arab Spring "The people want..." (الشعب يريد) template
- The modularity of this slogan — keeping "The people want" and completing it with different demands — made it the most exportable Arab Spring linguistic structure
- Sources: [Wikipedia: Arab Spring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab_Spring)

### Latin American Slogans

**Chile: "No son 30 pesos, son 30 años" (It's not 30 pesos, it's 30 years)**
- Origin: October 2019 metro fare protests in Santiago
- Brilliantly reframes a small trigger (30-peso fare increase) as the last straw of 30 years of neoliberal policy since Pinochet
- "Chile despertó" (Chile woke up) — complementary slogan asserting emergence from passivity
- Led to constitutional convention and referendum process (though draft constitution was later rejected)
- Sources: [Wikipedia: 2019-2020 Chilean protests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019%E2%80%932020_Chilean_protests)

**Argentina: "Ni Una Menos" (Not One [Woman] Less)**
- Origin: June 3, 2015 Buenos Aires, after murder of 14-year-old Chiara Paez
- Derived from 1995 phrase by Mexican poet Susana Chávez: "Ni una muerta más" (Not one more dead woman) — Chávez herself was assassinated in 2011
- Mobilized 200,000 in Buenos Aires; spread across Latin America
- Credited as catalyst for Argentina's legalization of first-trimester abortion (December 2020)
- Visual symbol: Green handkerchief (pañuelo verde) — became symbol of Latin American abortion rights
- Spread to: Peru 2016 (largest protest in Peruvian history), Mexico 2020 (women's strike), Puerto Rico 2021
- Sources: [Wikipedia: Ni una menos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ni_una_menos)

### African Movement Slogans

**South Africa: #FeesMustFall (2015-2016)**
- Origin: October 12, 2015 at University of Witwatersrand
- Hashtag format in English — South African student movements use English as lingua franca
- Connected to #RhodesMustFall (2015 decolonization movement)
- Result: No fee increases in 2016; President Zuma announced from Union Buildings
- Leader: Shaeera Kalla (Wits SRC President 2015)
- Sources: [Wikipedia: FeesMustFall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fees_Must_Fall)

**Nigeria: #EndSARS (2020)**
- Targeted Special Anti-Robbery Squad (SARS) police brutality
- Hashtag format, English, youth-driven
- Led to Lekki toll gate massacre (October 20, 2020)
- Demonstrated the "trigger event" pattern: specific police violence video → hashtag → mass mobilization
- Sources: [Wikipedia: End SARS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/End_SARS)

**Kenya: "Occupy Parliament" / Kenya Gen Z (2024)**
- Gen Z-led protests against controversial Finance Bill tax hikes
- Slogans: "Reject Finance Bill 2024," "Ruto Must Go"
- Part of broader global Gen Z protest wave; organized via TikTok and Twitter
- Successful in forcing withdrawal of Finance Bill
- Sources: [Wikipedia: Gen Z protests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gen_Z_protests)

### Asian Movement Slogans

**Hong Kong: "Five Demands, Not One Less" (五大訴求，缺一不可)**
- Origin: June 2019 anti-extradition bill protests
- The five demands: withdrawal of bill, retraction of "riot" classification, release of protesters, independent inquiry, universal suffrage
- "Liberate Hong Kong, revolution of our times" (光復香港，時代革命) — banned under National Security Law
- "We are not drowning. We are fighting." — visual imagery of resilience
- Largest protest: estimated 2 million on June 16, 2019

**Pacific Islands: "We are not drowning. We are fighting."**
- Pacific Climate Warriors slogan (350 Pacific, since 2011)
- Reframes Pacific Islanders from climate victims to climate agents
- 2014 Newcastle canoe blockade: 30 activists from 12 Pacific nations blocked world's largest coal port with traditional canoes
- Won 2020 Pax Christi International Peace Award
- Sources: [Wikipedia: Pacific Climate Warriors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pacific_Climate_Warriors)

**Sudan: "Freedom, Peace, Justice" / Sudanese Revolution (2018-2019)**
- Led by Sudanese Professionals Association (non-NGO infrastructure)
- MANSAM (Women of Sudanese Civic and Political Groups) — key women's organization
- Result: Overthrow of Omar al-Bashir (April 2019), though followed by military coup (2021)

## LANGUAGE TRANSLATION & ADAPTATION

*How movement slogans travel — and fail to travel — across cultures*

### Successful Translations

**"Woman, Life, Freedom" (Jin, Jîyan, Azadî)**
- Original: Kurdish language, from PKK women's movement
- Traveled to: Persian (زن، زندگی، آزادی / Zan, Zendegi, Azadi), Arabic, English, and 100+ languages
- Why it translated: Three universal concepts with no cultural specificity. Each word is independently meaningful in any language.
- Visual translation: Hair-cutting videos needed no words — the gesture translated instantly

**"We are the 99%"**
- Original: English, Occupy Wall Street
- Translated as a *concept* rather than a phrase — the 1%/99% mathematical framing worked because numbers don't need translation
- Spread to: Occupy movements in 82 countries
- Why it worked: Numbers are universal. The concept doesn't require cultural context.

**"Me Too"**
- Original: English, translated literally to 85+ countries
- Works because "me too" is a phrase that exists in every language — the grammar is universal
- The hashtag format (#MeToo) doesn't require translation at all — it travels as English and is understood

### Failed Translations

**"Black Lives Matter" in France → "Vies Noires Comptent"**
- Problem: France's official ideology of *universalism* (colorblindness) creates tension with race-conscious American framing
- Some French activists adopted BLM directly in English; others rejected it as American import
- Alternative framings: "Justice pour Adama" (Adama Traoré, killed by police 2016) — locally rooted
- Lesson: Race-conscious language doesn't travel to contexts where race is officially invisible

**"Defund the Police"**
- Failed to translate even within English-speaking countries
- In UK: "Defund" has different connotations; police structure is different
- The slogan requires understanding of American policing and municipal budgets to make sense
- Lesson: Policy-specific language doesn't travel; universal values do

**Arab Spring slogans in non-Arabic contexts**
- "The people want the fall of the regime" — requires Arabic language infrastructure
- "Bread, freedom, social justice" — the Arabic word *aish* (bread/life) doesn't carry the same dual meaning in other languages
- Lesson: Slogans that depend on linguistic features (puns, dual meanings) may not travel

### The Translation Spectrum

| Slogan | Translates? | Why |
|--------|-------------|-----|
| Woman, Life, Freedom | ✓ Perfectly | Universal concepts, no culture-specific content |
| Water is Life | ✓ Well | Universal metaphor (water = survival) |
| Me Too | ✓ Well | Exists in all languages |
| We are the 99% | ✓ Well | Numbers are universal |
| Black Lives Matter | ⚠️ Partial | Race-conscious framing is culturally specific |
| Defund the Police | ✗ Poorly | Policy-specific, requires context |
| Dégage! | ✗ Poorly | French imperative, culturally specific |
| Bread, Freedom, Justice | ⚠️ Partial | Concepts universal, but Arabic *aish* pun doesn't travel |

### Translation Strategy for SIPM

For Benjamin's global work:
1. **Test slogans for translation-independence** — Does the phrase require English-language context?
2. **Prefer visual slogans** — Images don't need translation. Hair-cutting translates. Canoes blocking coal ships translates.
3. **Avoid culture-specific metaphors** — "No kings" is deeply American; "freedom" is universal
4. **Use numbers and scale** — "92 million tons of textile waste" translates everywhere

## BRIDGE METAPHORS: Language That Connects Communities

*Slogans and images that bridge multiple movements or communities*

### Concept

A "bridge metaphor" is language or imagery that deliberately connects two or more communities who don't normally see themselves as part of the same movement. The most powerful bridges create "aha" moments where people realize they're fighting the same system.

### Examples of Successful Bridge Metaphors

**1. "I Can't Breathe" → Eric Garner → George Floyd → COVID → Climate**
- Original meaning: Police chokehold (Eric Garner 2014, George Floyd 2020)
- Extended meaning: Respirators and ventilators during COVID-19 — "I can't breathe" took on pandemic meaning
- Further extension: Air pollution, climate change — "I can't breathe" as environmental justice
- The phrase bridges: Police brutality, pandemic response, environmental racism
- How it bridged: The physical experience of breath deprivation is universal; the metaphor scales

**2. "Water is Life" / "Mni Wiconi" → Standing Rock → Flint → Pacific Islands**
- Original context: Standing Rock pipeline (indigenous water rights)
- Extended to: Flint water crisis (racial and economic water injustice)
- Further extended to: Pacific Island climate activism ("We are not drowning")
- The phrase bridges: Indigenous rights, environmental justice, racial justice, climate
- How it bridged: Water connects every human body to every ecosystem — the most universal bridge possible

**3. "Essential but Expendable" → Healthcare → Gig Economy → AI/Automation**
- Original context: COVID-19 essential workers (nurses, grocery workers)
- Extended to: Gig economy workers (Uber, DoorDash) — "essential" to platform profits, no benefits
- Further extension: AI replacement — workers essential until they can be automated
- The phrase bridges: Labor rights, healthcare, platform capitalism, AI displacement
- How it bridged: The contradiction (essential vs. expendable) applies to any devalued labor

**4. "Who Made My Clothes?" → Rana Plaza → Fast Fashion → E-Waste → AI Training**
- Original context: Rana Plaza collapse (2013), garment worker exploitation
- Extended to: Fast fashion waste (Atacama Desert)
- Further extended to: E-waste (Congo cobalt mining, e-waste dumping)
- Future extension: AI training data annotation (Kenyan workers labeling data for $2/hour)
- The phrase bridges: Labor exploitation, environmental destruction, tech supply chains
- How it bridged: The question "who made this?" applies to any hidden labor

### How Benjamin's Work Creates Bridges

Von Wong's installations naturally function as bridge metaphors:

**Mermaids in ocean plastic (2016)**
- Bridge: Marine conservation + plastic pollution + mythic beauty
- The mermaid is mythically associated with the sea; plastic pollution is destroying the sea
- The bridge: Beauty and destruction in the same frame

**Giant Tap of plastic bags (2018)**
- Bridge: Everyday consumer behavior + systemic waste + scale
- The tap makes the abstract ("millions of plastic bags per year") into a single comprehensible object
- The bridge: Individual action and industrial scale in one image

**Fashion Desert (if created)**
- Potential bridge: Fast fashion + climate + labor + waste
- A desert of clothes bridges: consumer choice, garment worker exploitation, carbon emissions, waste

### The Bridge Metaphor Toolkit

For SIPM, potential bridge metaphors:

| Bridge Phrase | Communities It Connects | Physical Image |
|---------------|------------------------|----------------|
| "The Invisible Supply Chain" | Tech workers + factory workers + miners + AI trainers | Hands passing objects down a chain, faces hidden |
| "Same System, Different Cage" | Refugees + gig workers + incarcerated people + animals | Interchangeable enclosures |
| "The Weight of the Invisible" | Carbon + surveillance data + unpaid labor | Person carrying invisible load made visible |
| "What They Throw Away" | Fashion waste + e-waste + people | Mountains of discarded items and displaced people |

### Cautions

Bridge metaphors fail when:
- They flatten important distinctions (equating unequal struggles can feel appropriative)
- They're too clever (if the connection requires explanation, it's not a bridge, it's a lecture)
- They lack permission (using one community's imagery to serve another requires relationship)

The best bridges are discovered, not invented — they emerge when communities realize they're already fighting the same fight.

## Pattern Analysis

### What do the most successful movement slogans have in common?

**1. Radical brevity — 6 words or fewer, ideally 3**
- "Black Lives Matter" (3 words)
- "Me Too" (2 words)
- "Love Wins" (2 words)
- "No Kings" (2 words)
- "Water is Life" (3 words)
- "Woman, Life, Freedom" (3 words)
- "Never Again" (2 words)
The shorter the phrase, the more portable it is — on a protest sign, a tweet, a t-shirt, a mural, shouted aloud. Length is friction.

**2. They make a claim, not just a demand**
The best slogans don't just say "stop doing X." They assert a truth about the world. "Black Lives Matter" is a statement about reality — one that the status quo contradicts. "Love Wins" asserted the conclusion before it happened. "Woman, Life, Freedom" is a philosophy. Claims are harder to argue against than demands; you have to either agree or reveal your position.

**3. They contain a tension or implied antagonist**
"I Can't Breathe" is a factual statement that becomes an accusation because we know who caused the inability to breathe. "Essential but Expendable" names the contradiction. "Defund the Police" had tension — but its tension was *internal to the movement*, which was fatal. The best tensions are between the movement and the system, not within the message itself.

**4. They are participatory — anyone can speak them**
"Me Too" — anyone can say this. "We are the 99%" — inclusive by definition. "Never Again" — any loss qualifies. The first-person plural ("we") or second-person ("you stole my dreams") creates immediate speaker position. Contrast with more technical demands ("Ban assault weapons," "Defund the police") which require the listener to already have a position.

**5. They have sonic texture — they work aloud**
"Black Lives Matter" — the stresses are on Lives and Matter, a natural chant. "Mni Wiconi" — rhythmic, repetitive syllables. "Jin, Jîyan, Azadî" — three syllables each, perfect chant structure. The best slogans are written for the throat, not the screen.

---

### What makes a hashtag spread vs. fizzle?

**Spreads:**
- **Low barrier to participation**: #MeToo required only two words. #BlackLivesMatter required conviction but not insider knowledge. #LoveWins required only joy.
- **Attached to a specific, dateable moment**: #BlackLivesMatter at Trayvon, Garza's post; #MeToo at the Weinstein exposé; #LoveWins at the Supreme Court ruling. A hashtag without a triggering moment rarely scales.
- **Enables personal testimony**: The most viral hashtags create a container for people to share their own experience (Me Too, Say Their Names). They're not just about a cause; they're about *the person sharing*.
- **Works visually**: A hashtag that attaches to an image travels further than one that attaches only to text. #NoDAPL + the attack dog images; #FridaysForFuture + Greta's strike sign.

**Fizzles:**
- **Too much explanation required**: If a second tweet is needed to explain the first, the hashtag has failed as a vehicle
- **Technically awkward**: Long, hard-to-spell, multi-word hashtags get corrupted in transmission
- **No clear in-group/out-group**: Hashtags that try to appeal to everyone (and thus offend no one) rarely go viral; the act of exclusion/inclusion creates energy
- **Attached only to a grievance, not a transformation**: Pure complaint without a vision of what victory looks like exhausts participants

---

### What visual metaphors work across cultures?

**Universal body metaphors**: Hands (raised, held up, choking), knees (kneeling), breath, hair. Bodies communicating emotions and politics simultaneously transcend language.

**Nature as stakeholder**: Water, forests, fire, sky. "Our house is on fire" works in every language. "Water is Life" — water doesn't belong to one culture. The Atacama clothing mountains work because the desert itself becomes a witness.

**Scale revealed through visual proof**: Aerial protest crowds, mountains of garbage, satellite images of destruction. The camera makes the invisible visible. When scale is literally shown (not described), it removes denial.

**Inversion and irony**: A pink sailboat in the middle of an intersection. A teenage girl outside a parliament alone. A police officer casually pepper-spraying seated students. The visual contradiction forces a question.

**The face of the specific**: Not "women killed by police" but Mahsa Amini's specific face; not "Parkland students" but X González's specific silence. The specific humanizes the abstract. This is why "Say their names" is powerful — it resists abstraction.

**Coded symbols (resistance under censorship)**: The watermelon 🍉 for Palestine, the rainbow for LGBTQ+. When mainstream expression is suppressed, communities develop alternative visual vocabularies. These coded symbols carry the additional layer of meaning: the fact of their coding proves the repression exists.

---

### What language caused movements to LOSE support?

1. **"Defund the Police"** — technically precise internally, catastrophically ambiguous externally. Lesson: never use a word your opponents can redefine more easily than you can define.

2. **"From the River to the Sea"** — geographically specific enough to require explanation but politically ambiguous enough to be weaponized in both directions. Phrases that require context to be understood as non-threatening are vulnerabilities.

3. **XR's disruptive tactics as own-brand liability** — when your action IS the message and your action alienates the people you're trying to convert, your actions become the story instead of your cause.

4. **"Hero" framing for essential workers** — the military-honor vocabulary ("heroes," "frontline warriors") was used in place of material recognition, making it a rhetorical substitute for wages and protections.

5. **"Genocide" as contested legal/political term** — accurate to many international legal scholars, but its contested status in mainstream Western political discourse made it a polarizing entry point rather than a unifying one for the Gaza movement.

6. **Generalized calls for systemic change without specific asks** — Occupy Wall Street had power but no demands. Power without a demand is theater without stakes.

### Lessons for Benjamin Von Wong's SIPM Project

**1. Choose the frame before the image**
Every great viral image is *inside* a larger frame. The Atacama clothing mountains work because "fast fashion is destroying the planet" is the frame. The image proves what the frame claims. Ask: what do you want people to believe after they see this? Work backward from that belief to the image.

**2. Make the contradiction visible**
The most powerful images show impossibility: a pink boat in traffic, a child's name written on their leg in case they die, clothing mountains in a pristine desert. The visual contradiction does the rhetorical work. What is the contradiction in SIPM that can be made visually explicit?

**3. Brevity is the highest discipline**
Three-word slogans outlast campaigns, funding cycles, and news cycles. The effort to get to three words is where the real thinking happens.

**4. Use the body**
Hair cutting, knee-taking, breath, fists — bodily metaphors cross all languages. If the SIPM work can connect to a physical gesture people can perform, the movement becomes embodied rather than conceptual.

**5. Design for the throat AND the screen**
The best slogans work as chants AND as hashtags AND as protest signs AND as 15-second Instagram videos. Test your language against all four.

**6. Avoid the "defund" trap**
Never use language that requires a corrective explanation. If your slogan's second sentence is "what we really mean is..." — find new language.

**7. Coded imagery creates community**
The watermelon works partly BECAUSE it's coded — it signals membership in a community of understanding. If SIPM develops visual language that rewards recognition, it creates belonging.

**8. Pair the global frame with the specific face**
The most powerful movement communication moves between scale (the systemic, the global) and the specific (one person's face, one person's story). Von Wong's installations often do this — the individual within the overwhelming system. This is the right instinct.
