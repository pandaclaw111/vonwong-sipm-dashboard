## Key Patterns Observed Across Cases 26–50

### 1. Economic Triggers Are Consistently Powerful (but Insufficient Alone)
Chile (#7, section A), Tunisia (#44), Lebanon (#48), Jordan (#50), Sri Lanka (#27), Thailand economic component (#28) — all show that economic shock triggers can produce massive mobilization but rarely produce structural change without institutional alternatives ready.

**Best example:** Sri Lanka achieved presidential removal but Wickremesinghe's election showed the political system could absorb the shock without structural reform. The Aragalaya's delayed win came through 2024 elections (Dissanayake), not the original protest.

### 2. The "Empty Vacuum" Problem (8 of 25 cases scored 0 or 1)
Movements that successfully removed leaders without having institutional alternatives ready consistently produced worse outcomes (Lebanon, Egypt, Myanmar, Haiti). The most common false-tip pattern in this batch.

**Worst case:** Haiti's movement correctly identified $2 billion theft; the political pressure it created contributed to instability rather than accountability.

### 3. Institutional Validators Determine Outcomes in Medium-Strength Cases
Guatemala without CICIG (0) vs. Guatemala with CICIG (3). The difference is the institutional validator—not the movement's strength, its narrative clarity, or its public support.

**Best illustration:** Taiwan (4) vs. Thailand (1). Both had clear demands, sophisticated organizations, and broad public support. Taiwan had institutional pathways (legislative processes, DPP electoral infrastructure). Thailand had none.

### 4. Visual Tactics Buy Time Against Censorship But Don't Determine Outcomes
Thailand's anime/meme tactics, Hong Kong's umbrellas, Iran's hair-cutting rituals—all generated global attention. None changed outcomes against states willing to use lethal force. Visual creativity is most effective against corporations and in democracies; it has a hard ceiling against authoritarian states.

### 5. The Long Reversal Pattern
Japan (nuclear: 2012 zero achieved → 2024 12 reactors running), Tunisia (2014 constitution → 2021 soft coup), Sri Lanka (2022 Rajapaksa removal → 2022 Wickremesinghe restoration → 2024 progressive win), Ecuador (2008 Rights of Nature → 2010s extraction expansion → 2022 Yasuni ruling)—many movements experience multi-phase cycles rather than linear progress or failure.

## Highest Outcome Cases (Score 4-5)

### Bolivia Cochabamba Water Wars (5/5)
- **Why it worked:** Clear single demand (reverse privatization), ready organizational infrastructure (Coordinadora), specific enemy (Bechtel), tactical escalation (general strike + roadblocks), government's only alternative was violent suppression with international audience watching
- **Lasting legacy:** "Water is a human right" frame → UN resolution 2010; Evo Morales election 2005; model for anti-privatization movements globally

### Bangladesh Rana Plaza (4/5)
- **Why it worked:** Undeniable physical evidence (brand labels in rubble), specific legal target (building safety), legally binding instrument (Accord—not just pledges), sustained international labor rights infrastructure
- **Honest caveat:** Wages remain inadequate; structural power imbalance unchanged; Accord needed renewal campaigns

### Taiwan Sunflower (4/5)
- **Why it worked:** Immediate political victory (CSSTA shelved), movement-to-party conversion (New Power Party), democratic context allowing electoral follow-through, threat was specific and legible
- **Honest caveat:** Long-term sovereignty threat from China remains existential; NPP experienced electoral decline after initial success

### Honduras/Cáceres (4/5)
- **Why it worked:** Goldman Prize visibility + assassination created international corporate accountability moment; three international financiers withdrew; dam canceled
- **Honest caveat:** Berta is dead; Honduras still world's most dangerous for environmental defenders

## Honest Assessments and Corrections

### Cases Inflated by Initial Reporting That Should Be Scored Lower:
- **Tunisia (3 not 4/5):** The Nobel Prize framing often inflates this case. Democratic reversal under Saied (2021) means the actual structural legacy is ambiguous. Scored 3 because the 2011-2021 period represented genuine cultural/institutional shift even if reversed.
- **Sri Lanka (3 not 4/5):** Gotabaya fled, but the economic reforms were driven by IMF not by the Aragalaya's demands. Dissanayake's 2024 election represents the movement's delayed political vindication but isn't a direct policy win from the protests.

### Cases Often Under-Discussed That Deserve Attention:
- **Ecuador Rights of Nature (3):** The 2022 Yasuni ruling and 2023 referendum represent real enforcement victories that post-Correa critics didn't expect. The framework is working better than cynics predicted.
- **Bolivia Water Wars (5):** Deserves much more prominent treatment in movement history; essentially flawless campaign design that achieved 100% of stated demands.
