### Current State (March 2026)
Australia holds the COP Presidency role with a Pre-COP summit planned in the Pacific Islands. Türkiye hosts the physical conference. Australia faces profound contradiction: it is the world's largest coal exporter, yet presents itself as a climate leader. The MAX Coalition (industry/finance/research) pushes Australia as a "renewable energy superpower." Civil society is mobilizing around the **Fossil Fuel Non-Proliferation Treaty** as a core demand.

## 2. Global Plastics Treaty — INC Negotiations 2026

### Timeline

| Date | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| Mar 2022 | UNEA-5.2 Resolution 5/14 passed | 175 nations mandate a global plastics treaty |
| Nov–Dec 2022 | INC-1, Punta del Este, Uruguay | First negotiating session |
| May–Jun 2023 | INC-2, Paris | Text development begins |
| Nov 2023 | INC-3, Nairobi | Production cuts emerge as fault line |
| Apr 2024 | INC-4, Ottawa | Petrostate blocking begins systematically |
| Nov–Dec 2024 | INC-5.1, Busan, South Korea | FAILED to reach agreement; petrostates block production cuts |
| Aug 2025 | INC-5.2, Geneva | Resumed session; Chair Luis Vayas still leading |
| Oct 2025 | Chair Vayas steps down | Process in crisis; new chair needed |
| Feb 7, 2026 | INC-5.3, Geneva (CICG) | Organizational/administrative session only — elected new chair |
| **2026 (TBD)** | **INC-6 — substantive session** | **The big fight; exact date TBD by new bureau** |
| 2026–2027 | Final treaty finalization | Or collapse if petrostates sustain bloc |

### Current State (March 2026)
The treaty is in deep crisis. After INC-5.1 collapsed in Busan, and the chair resigned in October 2025, a minimal INC-5.3 session on February 7, 2026 elected a new chair without substantive negotiations. An INC-6 session must be scheduled by the new bureau — likely mid-to-late 2026. The core fault line: **85+ countries support production cuts** (Rwanda-led coalition); **oil-producing states (Saudi Arabia, Iran, Russia, US under Trump)** are blocking.

### Key Organizations

**Civil Society (INSIDE the room):**
- **Break Free From Plastic** ([breakfreefromplastic.org](https://www.breakfreefromplastic.org)) — Global coalition of 11,000+ organizations; frontline communities, waste pickers, scientists; led INC monitoring — *Funded by: Wellspring Philanthropic Fund, Tides Foundation, others*
- **Center for International Environmental Law / CIEL** ([ciel.org](https://www.ciel.org)) — Legal analysis, treaty text advocacy, human rights framing — Contact: Andres del Castillo, Cate Bonacini +1-510-520-9109
- **GAIA (Global Alliance for Incinerator Alternatives)** ([no-burn.org](https://no-burn.org)) — Waste picker rights, incinerator opposition, zero-waste alternatives
- **Plastic Pollution Coalition** ([plasticpollutioncoalition.org](https://plasticpollutioncoalition.org)) — Communications, coalition building, US/global civil society

**Policy advocacy:**
- **Environmental Investigation Agency / EIA** ([eia-international.org](https://eia-international.org)) — Investigative research, petrostate tactics documentation

### Key Funders

- **Minderoo Foundation** (Andrew Forrest, Australia) — $300M+ commitment to end plastic pollution; funds advocacy and research
- **Bloomberg Philanthropies** — funds plastic pollution campaigns through Bloomberg Ocean Initiative
- **Wellspring Philanthropic Fund** — primary funder of Break Free From Plastic global secretariat
- **Oak Foundation** — environmental health, plastic/chemicals work
- **William and Flora Hewlett Foundation** — climate and environmental grants

### Petrostate Blocking Tactics (Documented)

1. **Fossil fuel lobbyists** flooded INC-5.1: 220+ lobbyists from plastic/petrochemical industries attended in Busan (November 2024), more than any single country delegation except host South Korea
2. **American Chemistry Council (ACC)** lobbying: ACC spent $9.9M on federal lobbying in 2023; pushed for treaty language limiting production controls; registered representatives inside INC sessions
3. **Saudi Arabia, Russia, Iran** formed a blocking coalition — used **consensus rule** to prevent passage of any production cut language
4. **Chair manipulation**: Chair Vayas was accused by civil society of producing successive draft texts (zero-draft, revised text) that weakened production cut language with each iteration
5. **Scope limitation gambit**: Petrostates pushed to limit treaty scope to "waste management" only — excluding production, design, chemical concerns

### The Panama Coalition (85 countries)
Led by Panama, this group includes Rwanda, Sierra Leone, the EU, Japan, and Pacific Island states — calling for a "systematic, adaptive approach" to reduce global plastic production. Their text proposal includes a global production reduction target with science-based timelines.

### Micro-movements building toward tip

- **Waste pickers' movement** (GAIA/WIEGO network, 20 million waste pickers globally) — demanding recognition and rights; increasingly visible at INC sessions
- **Plastic Health Council** — linking plastic chemicals (PFAS, phthalates, BPA) to human health; courts pursuing corporate liability cases
- **State-level plastic legislation** (California AB 2398, EU SUPD enforcement) — building pressure for global standard
- **Class action lawsuits** against plastic manufacturers (US, EU, Australia) — building parallel legal track
- **Petrochemical plant communities** — fenceline communities in Louisiana ("Cancer Alley"), Texas, South Korea demanding accountability

### Intersection Opportunities

- **COP31**: Plastics = 4% of global GHG emissions; plastic production drives fossil fuel demand; treaty and COP have same opponents
- **Loss & Damage**: Plastic pollution in Pacific/Caribbean small island states creates compound harm; bridge between funds
- **Health movements**: PFAS contamination lawsuits + plastic treaty = compound corporate accountability narrative

## 3. Loss & Damage Fund — The Gap Between Pledged and Needed

### Timeline

| Date | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| Nov 2022 | COP27 (Sharm el-Sheikh) | Landmark agreement to create L&D fund — 30-year fight wins |
| Dec 2023 | COP28 (Dubai) | Fund formally launched; initial pledges totaling ~$792M |
| Nov 2024 | COP29 (Baku) | $300B/yr NCQG agreed for 2035 — far short of $1.3T demanded; L&D buried |
| Nov 2025 | COP30 (Belém, Brazil) — "global mutirão" framework | Voluntary fossil fuel roadmaps (NOT binding); triple adaptation finance goal; L&D still underfunded; divisions deepen |
| Nov 2026 | COP31 (Antalya) | Australia as President — disbursement processes under scrutiny |
| 2027–2028 | First actual disbursements expected | Whether fund reaches communities or stays in bureaucracy |

### The Gap Problem

- **Pledged to fund**: ~$792 million (as of COP28) — US, Germany, UAE, UK top pledgers
- **IPCC estimate of annual L&D**: **$400–580 BILLION per year** (AR6 WG2)
- **Developing country demand**: $1.3 trillion/year (joint G77/China position at COP29)
- **COP29 outcome**: Countries "called for" raising $1.3T/yr from "wide sources including private investment" by 2035 — **not a commitment, a call**
- **Effective funding ratio**: ~0.1-0.2 cents of every dollar needed

### Fund Governance

- **Hosted by**: World Bank (interim, 4-year agreement, contested by civil society who see WB as pro-fossil infrastructure)
- **Board**: 26 members — 12 from developing countries, 2 from small island states, 2 from least developed countries, 10 from developed countries + EU; plus 2 civil society observer seats
- **Board Chair**: (changes annually)
- **Key disbursement battles**: 
  - Who defines "loss and damage" vs "adaptation" (line-blurring that lets rich countries double-count)
  - Direct access vs. through multilateral institutions
  - Non-economic losses (culture, tradition, land identity) — developing countries demand recognition; rich countries resist quantifying
  - **Debt trap concern**: Whether L&D comes as grants vs. loans

### Most Vocal Affected Countries

- **Vanuatu**: Pursuing ICJ advisory opinion on climate obligations of major emitters (landmark case)
- **Bangladesh**: 170M people, #1 vulnerability score; foreign minister repeatedly demands direct access
- **Pakistan**: 2022 floods caused $30B+ damage; received <$1B in assistance
- **Mozambique**: Cyclone Idai (2019), Kenneth (2019) — government demanding reparations framing
- **Tuvalu**: Potentially uninhabitable by 2070; signed "Treaty of Falepili" with Australia to enable migration
- **AOSIS (39 small island states)**: Unified bloc demanding loss and damage as climate justice, not charity

### Key Organizations

- **Climate Action Network International / CANI** ([climatenetwork.org](https://climatenetwork.org)) — Policy coordination, 1500+ member orgs — monitors fund governance
- **ActionAid International** ([actionaid.org](https://actionaid.org)) — Frontline communities, feminist climate justice; pushing direct access mechanisms
- **Loss and Damage Collaboration** ([lossanddamagecollaboration.org](https://lossanddamagecollaboration.org)) — Research collective on L&D policy (operational status unclear post-2025)
- **V20 Group** (Vulnerable Twenty) — Finance ministers of 58 climate-vulnerable countries; push for insurance and IMF-style access
- **Jubilee Debt Campaign** ([jubileedebt.org.uk](https://jubileedebt.org.uk)) — Framing L&D as debt justice, not charity

### Key Funders (of L&D advocacy)

- **Germany** — ~$100M pledged to fund + major philanthropic actor through GIZ
- **EU** — €225M pledged; also funds civil society monitoring
- **Open Society Foundations** — funds climate justice coalitions in Global South
- **Ford Foundation** — $1B "mission investments" in climate; includes L&D justice work

### Micro-movements building toward tip

- **ICJ Climate Advisory Opinion** — Vanuatu's landmark legal push; decision expected 2025-2026; would establish state responsibility in international law
- **Climate litigation surge**: 2,000+ climate cases in 65 countries (Grantham Institute 2023); building body of precedent
- **Debt justice movement** (Jubilee, CADTM): merging climate and debt narratives; pushing for automatic L&D financing when climate disasters occur
- **Frontline women's coalitions** (WEDO, GenderCC) — demanding gender-responsive disbursement criteria

### Intersection Opportunities

- **COP31**: Australia as President has direct leverage over fund governance decisions
- **Plastics Treaty**: Plastic pollution as a compounding harm in L&D–eligible countries; opportunity to link finance mechanisms
- **US midterms**: If Democrats flip Congress in 2026, potential for renewed US climate finance commitments

## 7. EU AI Act — August 2026 Enforcement

### Timeline

| Date | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| Apr 2021 | EU Commission proposes AI Act | World's first comprehensive AI regulation |
| Jun 2024 | AI Act formally adopted | Enters into force |
| **Feb 2, 2025** | **Banned AI applications take effect** | Cognitive manipulation, social scoring, real-time biometric ID in public spaces BANNED |
| Aug 2025 | GPAI (general-purpose AI) transparency rules apply | ChatGPT-style systems must disclose training data, prevent illegal content |
| **Aug 2, 2026** | **High-risk AI system rules apply** | The major enforcement trigger — affects AI in healthcare, education, employment, law enforcement, biometrics |
| Aug 2027 | Full Act applies to all systems | Including lower-risk systems |
| 2028 | First major enforcement actions expected | National regulators + EU AI Office prosecuting cases |

### What "High-Risk AI" Means Concretely

Systems that must register in EU database and comply with conformity assessment:
- **Employment/HR AI**: CV screening, hiring algorithms (LinkedIn, SAP, Oracle, Workday)
- **Education AI**: Student assessment, admission systems
- **Credit/insurance scoring**: AI credit decisions affecting access to essential services
- **Law enforcement AI**: Predictive policing, criminal risk assessment
- **Border control AI**: Facial recognition at EU borders
- **Critical infrastructure AI**: Power grids, water systems managed by AI
- **Medical device AI**: Diagnostics, treatment recommendation

**Prohibited (already in effect since Feb 2025):**
- Real-time remote biometric identification in public spaces (except narrow law enforcement exceptions)
- Social scoring (China-style)
- Emotion recognition in workplaces and schools
- AI that exploits vulnerabilities of children/disabled people

### Fines Structure

| Violation | Maximum Fine |
|-----------|-------------|
| Prohibited AI use | **€35 million or 7% of global annual turnover** |
| High-risk non-compliance | €15 million or 3% global turnover |
| Providing false information to authorities | €7.5 million or 1.5% global turnover |

### Enforcement Bodies

- **EU AI Office** (created Feb 2024 under European Commission) — Enforces GPAI model rules; coordinates national authorities
- **National Market Surveillance Authorities (MSAs)** — Each EU member state has designated authority (e.g., Germany: Federal Network Agency; France: ANSSI/CNIL)
- **European Parliament Working Group on AI Act Implementation** — Legislative oversight

### Key Civil Society Organizations

- **AlgorithmWatch** ([algorithmwatch.org](https://algorithmwatch.org)) — German-based; monitors automated systems discrimination; doing pre-compliance audits on HR AI
- **Access Now** ([accessnow.org](https://accessnow.org)) — Digital rights; monitoring biometric surveillance enforcement; EU policy advocacy
- **EDRi (European Digital Rights)** ([edri.org](https://edri.org)) — 45+ member organizations; coordinated campaign to strengthen enforcement; pushed for stronger banned AI list
- **Foxglove** ([foxglove.legal](https://foxglove.legal)) — UK/EU legal clinic; litigating algorithmic harm cases
- **AI Now Institute** ([ainowinstitute.org](https://ainowinstitute.org)) — Research; early warning system for AI governance failures

### Key Funders

- **Mozilla Foundation** — $100M+ for AI accountability; funds EDRi and AlgorithmWatch
- **Open Society Foundations** — €50M+ for digital rights in Europe
- **Google, Microsoft, Meta** fund compliant civil society groups (conflicts of interest documented)

### Micro-movements building toward tip

- **Workers' algorithmic rights movement** — UNI Global Union and national unions demanding AI transparency in hiring/firing; direct challenge to GPAI systems
- **Predictive policing lawsuits** — Dutch courts challenged (and banned) SyRI welfare fraud AI 2020; precedent being applied to enforcement AI
- **Facial recognition bans** — Cities like Brussels, Amsterdam, Hamburg imposing local moratoriums; building pressure for strict enforcement
- **Healthcare AI accountability** — Patient groups demanding explanation rights for AI diagnostics

## 8. Section 702 FISA Reauthorization (April 2026)

### Timeline

| Date | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|-------------|
| Sep 2001 | Post-9/11 surveillance expansion | PATRIOT Act; building block |
| 2008 | FISA Amendments Act / Section 702 created | Legalizes warrantless surveillance of foreign targets (with US "incidental" collection) |
| 2013 | Snowden revelations | Expose PRISM program; public awareness |
| Apr 2024 | Section 702 reauthorized for 2 more years | Also **expanded**: "any business that can access communications" added; EFF calls it "shameful" |
| Jan 2025 | Federal court rules backdoor searches of 702 data unconstitutional | First major legal victory for reformers |
| May 2025 | Montana closes data broker loophole (first state) | Model legislation for federal SAFE Act |
| Feb 2026 | Trump White House calls for "clean reauthorization" (no reforms) | Sets up the battle |
| **Mar 2026** | **SAFE Act introduced** by Senators Mike Lee (R-UT) and Dick Durbin (D-IL) | Bipartisan reform bill — includes warrant requirements, data broker closure, parallel construction ban |
| **Apr 2026** | **Section 702 EXPIRES** | Congress must act; battle lines drawn |
| 2026–2027 | Outcome shapes US surveillance state | Either reformed or expanded again |

### Current State (March 2026)
Section 702 expires in April 2026. Multiple proposals in play:
1. **Trump White House**: Clean reauthorization — no changes, 2 more years
2. **SAFE Act (Lee/Durbin)**: Bipartisan reform — warrant requirements for FBI content searches, data broker loophole closure, parallel construction ban
3. **Civil libertarians (EFF, ACLU)**: Want NO reauthorization without major reforms
4. **Intelligence community (NSA/CIA/FBI)**: Pushing hardest for clean extension or minimal changes

**What makes 2026 different from 2024:**
- Federal court ruling (Jan 2025) that backdoor searches are unconstitutional gives reformers judicial support
- Under Trump, Republicans more wary of surveillance being turned on THEM
- January 6 prosecutions (FBI used surveillance tools on Americans) increased GOP skepticism
- DOGE/government transparency movement on right converging with civil liberties left

### What's at Stake

- **3.4 million warrantless FBI searches** of Americans' 702-collected data in 2021 alone
- **Foreign intelligence collection** sweeps in Americans' communications without warrant
- **Data broker loophole**: FBI/NSA can BUY your location data from brokers without a warrant
- **Parallel construction**: Law enforcement hides how they obtained information (no disclosure of 702 use)
- **Scope creep**: 2024 reauthorization added "any business with equipment" = potential for landlords, hotels, ISPs, etc. to become surveillance infrastructure

### Organizations (Reform Side)

- **Electronic Frontier Foundation / EFF** ([eff.org](https://www.eff.org)) — Litigation + policy; wrote detailed SAFE Act analysis (Mar 2026) — *Budget: $25M+; funded by major tech donors and small donors*

### Organizations (Status Quo / Expansion Side)

### Key Funders (Reform Side)

### Plastics Treaty from Waste Picker Perspective

**Who are waste pickers?**
- 20 million people globally live by collecting, sorting, and selling recyclable waste
- Majority in Global South: India (4 million), Brazil, Colombia, Indonesia, Philippines
- They perform 50-60% of all recycling in many countries — unpaid environmental labor
- Mostly women, marginalized castes, undocumented workers

**What waste pickers are demanding at INC negotiations:**
- Recognition of their labor as essential to waste management systems
- Just transition provisions if treaty reduces plastic production (their livelihoods depend on waste)
- Seats at the negotiating table — not just as observers but as stakeholders
- Formalization and fair wages instead of informal scavenging

**Organizations:**
- Global Alliance of Waste Pickers (GAIA/WIEGO network)
- Women in Informal Employment: Globalizing and Organizing (WIEGO)
- SWaCH Cooperative (Pune, India) — 3,000+ waste picker members

**The irony:**
- Waste pickers are the world's most efficient recyclers
- A treaty that focuses only on "waste management" would formalize systems that exploit them
- A treaty that focuses on production cuts would reduce waste, potentially harming their livelihoods
- They need BOTH recognition AND transition support
